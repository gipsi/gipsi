Aldo Leopold

The man who called for a Land Ethic

Here's a time line of Aldo Leopold's life:

  • 1887 - Born and raised in Burlington, Iowa, US, Aldo Leopold developed an interest in the natural world at an early age spending hours observing, journaling, and sketching his surroundings.
  • 1909 - He graduated from Lawrenceville Forest School, Yale University then eagerly pursued a career with the newly established US Forest Service in Arizona and New Mexico.
  • 1921 - Promoted to the post of supervisor for the Carson National Forest in New Mexico.
  • 1922 - Instrumental in developing the proposal to manage the Gila National Forest as a wilderness area until became the country’s first official wilderness area in 1924
  • 1924 - Transfered to Madison, Wisconsin where he continued to investigate ecology and the philosophy of conservation.
  • 1933 - Published the first textbook in the field of wildlife management. Accepted a new chair position in game management – a first for the University of Wisconsin and the US.
  • 1935 - Leopold and his family initiated their own ecological restoration experiment on a worn-out farm along the Wisconsin River outside of Baraboo, Wisconsin. They called it “the Shack,” and planted thousands of pine trees and restored prairies, he documented changes in the flora and fauna which further informed and inspired him.
  • 1936 - 1948 - He authored many articles for journals and magazines, and started to compile a book for general audiences examining humanity’s relationship to the natural world. Just a week after receiving word that his manuscript would be published, Leopold died of a heart attack on April 21, 1948.
    • 1949 - A Sand County Almanac was first published by Oxford University Press. It serves as one of the cornerstones of modern conservation science, policy, and ethics.

"Nothing so important as a land ethic is ever written... it evolves in the minds of a thinking community."

-- Aldo Leopold
